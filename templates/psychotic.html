Psychotic Disorders

“If you are a person with mental illness, the challenge is to find the life that's right for you."

Psychosis is a disconnection from reality. People may have false beliefs or experience things that aren’t real. Psychosis isn’t a condition; it describes a collection of symptoms.

Two important types of psychosis include:

  • Hallucinations: When parts of your brain mistakenly act like your senses (vision, hearing, touch, smell, and taste) are picking up something that's not actually there. Example: hearing voices that aren't present.
  • Delusions: False beliefs that someone holds onto very strongly, even when others disagree or there's clear evidence the belief isn’t true. Example: believing someone is controlling their thoughts or actions remotely.

Possible Causes

What are the most common causes of psychosis?

Psychosis is a symptom of many mental health conditions. The DSM-5 has a category dedicated to "Schizophrenia Spectrum and Other Psychotic Disorders," which includes:

  • Schizophrenia
  • Brief psychotic disorder
  • Delusional disorder
  • Schizoaffective disorder
  • Schizophreniform disorder
  • Schizotypal (personality) disorder
  • Substance/medication-induced psychotic disorder
  • Psychotic disorder due to another medical condition

Psychosis can also occur with certain mood disorders like:

  • Bipolar disorder
  • Major depression and related conditions

Medical conditions that can cause psychosis:

  • Alzheimer’s disease and other types of dementia
  • Hormone-related conditions (Addison’s disease, Cushing’s disease, thyroid disorders)
  • Infections (encephalitis, meningitis)
  • Lupus
  • Lyme disease
  • Multiple sclerosis
  • Postpartum psychosis
  • Stroke and other neurological conditions
  • Vitamin B1 (thiamine) and vitamin B12 deficiencies

Other causes of psychosis:

  • Misuse of alcohol, prescription medications, or recreational drugs
  • Severe head injuries
  • Traumatic experiences (past or present)
  • High levels of stress or anxiety

Care and Treatment

How is psychosis treated?

Treatment depends on the underlying cause. For direct psychosis treatment, there are several approaches:

  • Medications: Antipsychotic drugs, antidepressants, or lithium may be used.
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT): This can help manage conditions that cause or worsen psychosis.
  • Inpatient treatment: For severe cases where a person poses a danger to themselves or others.
  • Support programs: These help manage psychosis caused by other disorders (e.g., substance use, personality disorders).

How can psychosis be prevented?

  • Avoid recreational marijuana use, especially in teenage years and early adulthood.
  • Wear safety equipment to prevent head injuries.
  • Treat infections promptly, especially those affecting eyes and ears.
  • Maintain a balanced diet and healthy weight to reduce stroke risk, which can lead to psychosis.

When To Call the Doctor

If psychosis symptoms arise, consult a doctor or healthcare provider immediately. Conditions like stroke can be life-threatening.

Managing psychosis:

People with psychosis may have anosognosia, meaning they lack insight into their condition. It’s crucial to follow healthcare provider recommendations even if you don’t think anything is wrong.

Additional Common Questions

What is the difference between psychosis and schizophrenia?

Psychosis: A collection of symptoms involving a disconnection from reality. Can happen with many medical conditions.

Schizophrenia: A condition where psychosis is a key symptom.

What is the difference between psychosis and neurosis?

Psychosis: Refers to disconnection from reality.

Neurosis: An outdated term for high levels of anxiety. Doesn’t involve disconnection from reality.

Are there different kinds of psychosis?

There are specific conditions that use the term "psychosis," such as brief psychotic disorder and postpartum psychosis.

Types of delusions:

  • Bizarre: Extreme delusions that aren’t possible, e.g., believing a robot has replaced a loved one.
  • Non-bizarre: Possible but unsupported beliefs, e.g., believing someone is following them.

Types of hallucinations:

  • Auditory: Hearing voices that aren't there.
  • Visual: Seeing things that aren’t real.
  • Tactile: Feeling sensations that aren’t happening, such as crawling on the skin.

Warning Signs of Psychosis

Early warning signs can include emotional changes, behavioral shifts, and social withdrawal.

Helping a Loved One with Psychotic Symptoms

  • Listen and offer support.
  • Encourage them to seek help from a professional.
  • Stay calm and avoid arguing about their beliefs.
  • Get help in emergencies, especially if they express suicidal thoughts.
