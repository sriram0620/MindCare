Neurocognitive Disorder

“ Alzheimer’s is not about the past—the success, the accolades, the accomplishments… ”

Neurocognitive disorders involve a decline in cognitive functioning that is greater than what might be expected with normal aging. They affect a person's ability to think, reason, remember, and perform everyday activities, often leading to significant disruptions in their work, relationships, and quality of life.

These disorders range from mild cognitive impairments to severe forms like dementia, and they may be caused by factors like brain trauma, stroke, infections, or degenerative diseases.

What are the types of neurocognitive disorders?

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5) recognizes several types of neurocognitive disorders. These can be classified by the underlying cause and severity of cognitive decline. NCDs are generally categorized into the following:

Major neurocognitive disorder

Major neurocognitive disorder, previously known as dementia, is marked by significant cognitive decline from a previous level of functioning. This decline impairs the individual's ability to live independently and affects multiple cognitive domains, such as memory, language, and executive functioning.

Mild neurocognitive disorder

Mild neurocognitive disorder is characterized by a more modest cognitive decline that does not significantly interfere with independent functioning but may require some compensatory strategies. People with mild NCD may notice memory lapses or difficulty concentrating, but they can still manage most of their daily activities.

Specific types of neurocognitive disorders

There are several specific forms of neurocognitive disorders, often related to an underlying medical or neurological condition, including:

  • Alzheimer’s disease: The most common cause of neurocognitive decline, leading to memory loss, confusion, and changes in mood or personality.
  • Vascular neurocognitive disorder: Caused by reduced blood flow to the brain, often due to strokes or other vascular conditions.
  • Lewy body dementia: Characterized by abnormal protein deposits in the brain, causing visual hallucinations, movement difficulties, and fluctuating cognitive abilities.
  • Frontotemporal dementia: Affects the frontal and temporal lobes of the brain, leading to changes in behavior, personality, and language skills.
  • Traumatic brain injury (TBI): Can result in cognitive decline following an injury to the brain, leading to issues with attention, memory, and executive functioning.
  • Parkinson’s disease: While primarily a movement disorder, Parkinson’s can also cause cognitive decline in its later stages.
  • HIV infection: May lead to neurocognitive impairment due to direct effects of the virus on the brain.
  • Substance/medication-induced neurocognitive disorder: Cognitive decline due to the use or withdrawal from certain substances or medications.

Who do neurocognitive disorders affect?

Neurocognitive disorders can affect anyone, but certain factors increase the likelihood of developing these conditions:

  • Age: The risk of neurocognitive disorders increases significantly with age, particularly for disorders like Alzheimer's.
  • Gender: Some neurocognitive disorders, like Alzheimer’s, are more prevalent in people assigned female at birth, while others, like Parkinson’s disease, affect more people assigned male at birth.
  • Family history: Having a family history of neurodegenerative diseases can increase the risk.
  • Trauma and medical conditions: Brain injuries, strokes, and certain infections can lead to the development of neurocognitive disorders.

How common are neurocognitive disorders?

Neurocognitive disorders are quite common, especially among older adults. Alzheimer’s disease, for instance, is the most prevalent form of dementia, affecting millions of people worldwide. Vascular neurocognitive disorder is also common, particularly in those with a history of cardiovascular disease.

The prevalence of neurocognitive disorders increases significantly with age, with nearly 10% of people over the age of 65 and about 30% of those over 85 showing signs of some form of cognitive impairment.

Symptoms and Causes

What causes neurocognitive disorders?

The causes of neurocognitive disorders vary widely depending on the specific type. Some common factors include:

  • Age-related degeneration: Many neurocognitive disorders, such as Alzheimer's and frontotemporal dementia, are linked to aging and the natural degeneration of brain cells.
  • Vascular conditions: Strokes or other issues that affect blood flow to the brain can lead to vascular cognitive impairment.
  • Genetic factors: Genetic mutations or family history may increase the risk of neurocognitive disorders, especially in conditions like Alzheimer’s disease.
  • Injury or trauma: Physical injury to the brain, such as a traumatic brain injury, can result in cognitive impairments.
  • Substance use: Long-term use of substances such as alcohol or drugs can lead to cognitive decline.
  • Neurological conditions: Diseases like Parkinson’s, multiple sclerosis, or Huntington’s disease often involve cognitive impairments as part of the disease progression.

What are the symptoms of neurocognitive disorders?

The symptoms of neurocognitive disorders depend on the specific type and severity of the disorder but generally include:

  • Memory loss: Difficulty remembering recent events or familiar information.
  • Confusion: Disorientation regarding time, place, or personal identity.
  • Language difficulties: Trouble finding the right words or understanding language.
  • Impaired judgment: Difficulty making decisions or solving problems.
  • Personality changes: Mood swings, irritability, or changes in behavior.
  • Difficulty with everyday tasks: Trouble performing activities that were previously routine, such as managing finances or household chores.

How do you know if someone has a neurocognitive disorder?

Neurocognitive disorders can be difficult to diagnose, as some symptoms might initially resemble normal aging. However, when the decline is more significant and affects daily functioning, a neurocognitive disorder may be suspected.

The diagnosis is typically made by healthcare professionals through:

  • Cognitive testing: Assessments to evaluate memory, attention, language, and executive function.
  • Neuroimaging: MRI or CT scans to identify brain changes or damage.
  • Medical history: Evaluating the individual's medical history and potential risk factors.
  • Family input: Reports from family members or caregivers who have observed changes in the individual's behavior or cognitive abilities.

Diagnosis and Tests

How are neurocognitive disorders diagnosed?

To diagnose neurocognitive disorders, healthcare professionals use a combination of approaches:

  • Cognitive screening tools: Tests like the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) or the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) help determine the extent of cognitive impairment.
  • Neuroimaging: Brain scans (CT or MRI) are used to identify underlying causes such as strokes, tumors, or structural changes in the brain.
  • Laboratory tests: Blood tests may be conducted to rule out other causes like vitamin deficiencies or thyroid issues.

A thorough assessment includes a review of the individual's medical, psychiatric, and family history, as well as input from family members or caregivers.

Management and Treatment

How are neurocognitive disorders treated?

While there is no cure for most neurocognitive disorders, treatment focuses on managing symptoms and slowing the progression of the disease. Common approaches include:

  • Medications: Drugs such as cholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., donepezil) and memantine may help with memory and cognition in Alzheimer’s disease.
  • Therapy: Cognitive rehabilitation or occupational therapy may assist individuals in maintaining cognitive function and improving daily living skills.
  • Lifestyle changes: Physical exercise, healthy eating, and mental stimulation can help maintain brain health and slow cognitive decline.
  • Support: Emotional support from family, caregivers, and support groups plays a crucial role in managing the impact of neurocognitive disorders.
