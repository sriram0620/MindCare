Trauma and Stress Related Disorders

"To live in the body of a survivor is to never be able to leave the scene of the crime. I cannot ignore the fact that I live here. "

Trauma and stress related disorders involve mental health conditions that develop after exposure to a traumatic or highly stressful event. These disorders affect emotional, psychological, and physical well-being, and the symptoms often disrupt a person’s ability to function normally in everyday life.

Types of Trauma and Stress Related Disorders

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

PTSD occurs after a person experiences or witnesses a life-threatening or disturbing event such as military combat, assault, natural disasters, or severe accidents.

  • Symptoms: Intrusive thoughts like flashbacks, nightmares, emotional numbness, hypervigilance, difficulty sleeping, and irritability. Individuals may avoid places or people that remind them of the trauma.
  • Onset: Symptoms typically appear within 3 months of the traumatic event but can sometimes appear years later.
  • Duration: Symptoms must persist for more than a month for a PTSD diagnosis.

Acute Stress Disorder (ASD)

ASD is a short-term condition that occurs immediately following a traumatic event. Its symptoms are similar to PTSD but are more transient.

  • Symptoms: Dissociation, intrusive memories, emotional distress, avoidance, and heightened arousal.
  • Onset: Symptoms appear within days of the trauma.
  • Duration: Usually lasts from 3 days to 4 weeks. If symptoms persist longer than a month, it may be diagnosed as PTSD.

Adjustment Disorder

This disorder occurs when a person struggles to cope with a specific stressor, such as divorce, job loss, or a significant life change. It can affect both adults and children.

  • Symptoms: Anxiety, depression, social withdrawal, inability to focus, and physical symptoms such as headaches or stomachaches.
  • Onset: Symptoms typically begin within 3 months of the stressor.
  • Duration: Symptoms usually resolve once the stressor is removed or the person adapts to the situation.

Reactive Attachment Disorder (RAD)

RAD develops in young children who experience extreme neglect or disruptions in their caregiving. It is marked by difficulty forming emotional attachments.

  • Symptoms: Emotional withdrawal, lack of responsiveness to comfort, and failure to seek out social interactions. Children may appear sad, irritable, or fearful even in non-threatening situations.
  • Onset: Symptoms are observed in infancy or early childhood.
  • Duration: The disorder can persist if untreated, affecting emotional development and relationships throughout life.

Disinhibited Social Engagement Disorder (DSED)

DSED is a condition where children, typically those who have experienced severe neglect or inconsistent caregiving, show a lack of hesitation in engaging with unfamiliar adults.

  • Symptoms: Overly familiar behavior with strangers, lack of appropriate social boundaries, and eagerness to seek attention from unfamiliar individuals.
  • Onset: Symptoms typically appear in early childhood, after a period of neglect or inconsistent caregiving.
  • Duration: Without intervention, DSED can continue into adolescence, affecting relationships and behavior.

Who do Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders Affect?

These disorders can affect anyone who has experienced significant trauma or stress, including people of all ages, genders, and backgrounds. However, certain groups, such as military veterans, survivors of abuse, or people living in high-risk environments, may be more vulnerable.

Children who experience neglect, abuse, or prolonged separations from caregivers are particularly at risk for developing disorders like RAD or DSED.

How Common Are Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders?

  • PTSD affects approximately 6-7% of the U.S. population at some point in their lives.
  • Acute stress disorder is less commonly diagnosed due to its short duration, but it’s estimated that 13-21% of trauma survivors develop ASD.
  • Adjustment disorders are relatively common, particularly following significant life stressors.
  • RAD and DSED are more frequently diagnosed in children from orphanages, foster care, or those who have experienced significant neglect.

Symptoms and Causes of Trauma- and Stressor-Related Disorders

Common causes include:

  • Trauma: A direct exposure to a traumatic event, such as war, natural disasters, physical or sexual assault, accidents, or witnessing death.
  • Stressful life events: Non-traumatic but significant life changes like divorce, job loss, financial crises, or illness can lead to adjustment disorders.
  • Neglect or inadequate caregiving: Especially in the cases of RAD and DSED, childhood neglect or inconsistent caregiving causes disrupted emotional development.
  • Brain changes: Studies have shown that trauma can alter brain structures, such as the amygdala, which is responsible for fear responses, contributing to these disorders.

Common Symptoms

  • Intrusive thoughts: Flashbacks, nightmares, or uncontrollable memories of the traumatic event.
  • Emotional distress: Anxiety, fear, depression, guilt, or emotional numbness.
  • Avoidance: Staying away from places, people, or situations that trigger memories of the trauma.
  • Behavioral changes: Hypervigilance, irritability, anger outbursts, or reckless behavior.
  • Dissociation: Feeling detached from oneself or one’s surroundings, particularly in ASD and PTSD.
  • Physical symptoms: Headaches, stomachaches, and trouble sleeping, particularly in adjustment disorders.

Diagnosis and Treatment

To diagnose trauma- and stressor-related disorders, mental health professionals use the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5). The diagnostic process includes:

  • Interviews: To gather information about the person’s experiences and symptoms.
  • Questionnaires and screening tools: To assess the severity of symptoms.
  • Observation: In cases like RAD or DSED, observing how children interact with caregivers and others may provide diagnostic insight.

Treatment Options

Treatment methods include:

  • Psychotherapy: Talk therapy is the primary treatment for trauma- and stressor-related disorders. Types include Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR), and Play or family therapy for children with RAD or DSED.
  • Medications: Antidepressants, anti-anxiety medications, or sleep aids may be prescribed to alleviate symptoms like anxiety or insomnia.
  • Support groups: These can provide peer support and coping strategies for people with PTSD or adjustment disorders.
  • Parent-child interaction therapy (PCIT): Particularly helpful for children with RAD or DSED, PCIT focuses on improving attachment and caregiving behaviors.
