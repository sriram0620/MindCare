Somatic Symptom Disorder

“The mind and body are not separate. What affects one, affects the other.”

Somatic Symptom Disorder (SSD) involves a person having a significant focus on physical symptoms, such as pain, weakness, or shortness of breath, that results in major distress and/or problems functioning. The individual has excessive thoughts, feelings, and behaviors relating to the physical symptoms. They may:

  • Frequently visit healthcare providers for various physical complaints, even when no medical diagnosis is confirmed.
  • Believe that their symptoms are more severe than medical findings suggest.
  • Have anxiety about their health, frequently thinking that normal body sensations are signs of severe illness.

Symptoms and Causes

What Are the Signs and Symptoms of Somatic Symptom Disorder?

People with Somatic Symptom Disorder (SSD) experience various physical and psychological symptoms. Common physical symptoms include:

  • Pain (the most commonly reported symptom).
  • Fatigue or weakness.
  • Shortness of breath (dyspnea).

The severity of physical symptoms can vary from mild to severe, and individuals may experience one or multiple symptoms. These symptoms may be linked to a medical condition or may not have a clear medical cause.

In addition to physical symptoms, people with SSD exhibit distinct emotional and behavioral responses. These include:

  • Feeling extreme anxiety about their physical symptoms.
  • Believing that mild symptoms are signs of serious conditions.
  • Consulting healthcare providers for multiple diagnostic tests and exams but remaining skeptical of the results.
  • Feeling that their healthcare provider doesn’t take their symptoms seriously enough.
  • Visiting multiple healthcare providers or seeking treatment from various sources simultaneously.
  • Spending excessive time and energy on health concerns.
  • Being unusually sensitive to drug side effects.
  • Becoming dependent on others for emotional support, and feeling angry when they believe their needs aren’t met.
  • Having trouble functioning in daily life due to thoughts, feelings, and behaviors regarding their symptoms.

It’s important to note that approximately 30% to 60% of people with Somatic Symptom Disorder also experience anxiety and/or depression.

What Causes Somatic Symptom Disorder?

Researchers suggest that several biological, environmental, and psychological factors can contribute to the development of SSD. These factors include:

  • Childhood physical and sexual abuse.
  • Poor emotional awareness or development during childhood, often due to parental neglect or a lack of emotional closeness.
  • Excessive anxiety and attention to bodily processes and potential signs of illness.

Risk Factors

Factors that may increase the risk of developing Somatic Symptom Disorder include:

  • Having a family member with a history of SSD or anxiety disorders.
  • Experiencing stressful life events or trauma.
  • Being highly attentive to bodily sensations and misinterpreting them as serious medical issues.
  • Having a coexisting mental health condition such as depression or panic disorder.

Diagnosis and Tests

How Is Somatic Symptom Disorder Diagnosed?

The process of diagnosing Somatic Symptom Disorder (SSD) typically involves several steps. Your healthcare provider will likely:

  • Perform a physical exam to rule out any underlying medical conditions.
  • Order laboratory tests to check for conditions that could be causing your physical symptoms.
  • Ask about your health history.

If test results do not indicate the presence of a medical condition, or if the medical condition present does not align with the severity of your symptoms, your healthcare provider may refer you to a mental health provider for further evaluation.

A mental health provider, such as a psychologist or psychiatrist, will diagnose SSD based on specific criteria:

  • Having one or more symptoms that cause significant distress or disrupt daily life for at least six months.
  • Experiencing excessive thoughts, feelings, or behaviors related to the symptoms, which meet at least one of the following criteria:
    • Overly excessive and persistent thoughts about the seriousness of the symptoms.
    • Constantly high levels of anxiety regarding health or physical symptoms.
    • Spending an extreme amount of time and energy focused on physical symptoms and health concerns.

It can be challenging for people with Somatic Symptom Disorder to accept that their concerns about their symptoms are excessive.

Treatment and Management

How Is Somatic Symptom Disorder Treated?

The goal of treating Somatic Symptom Disorder is to manage both the physical and psychological symptoms through psychotherapy (talk therapy) and, in some cases, medications to treat underlying anxiety and depression.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is a common type of psychotherapy used to treat Somatic Symptom Disorder. CBT helps individuals change their patterns of thinking and behavior to alter the way they feel. Under the guidance of a psychologist or psychiatrist, CBT helps people better cope with anxiety, stress, and respond to situations more effectively.

If you have Somatic Symptom Disorder, CBT can help you:

  • Evaluate your feelings and beliefs about your physical symptoms and health in general.
  • Find ways to reduce stress and anxiety about your symptoms.
  • Stop focusing as much on your physical symptoms.
  • Recognize what seems to make your physical symptoms worse.
  • Find ways to stay active and social, even if you still have pain or other symptoms.
  • Function better in your daily life.

In addition to CBT, your healthcare provider may prescribe medication, such as antidepressants. While these medications primarily help with mood, they have also been reported to alleviate certain physical symptoms, including:

  • Pain
  • Fatigue
  • Joint pain
  • Sleep problems

By combining psychotherapy and medication, many individuals with Somatic Symptom Disorder can experience significant relief from their symptoms and improvements in their overall quality of life.

Prognosis

What Are the Long-Term Outcomes?

With treatment, many people with Somatic Symptom Disorder can experience significant improvement in their quality of life. However, untreated SSD can lead to:

  • Ongoing health anxiety and frequent use of healthcare services.
  • Impairment in daily functioning due to preoccupation with symptoms.
  • Increased risk of developing other mental health disorders, such as depression or anxiety.

Prevention

How Can Somatic Symptom Disorder Be Prevented?

There are several steps that may help reduce the risk of developing Somatic Symptom Disorder or prevent its recurrence. These include:

  • Identifying and addressing stress or trauma early on.
  • Encouraging the development of healthy coping strategies for stress and anxiety.
  • Seeking prompt treatment for underlying mental health conditions like depression or anxiety.
  • Maintaining regular follow-up care with a trusted healthcare provider to monitor symptoms.
  • Practicing relaxation techniques, such as mindfulness or meditation, to manage stress.
  • Learning to recognize and challenge catastrophic thoughts about physical symptoms.
